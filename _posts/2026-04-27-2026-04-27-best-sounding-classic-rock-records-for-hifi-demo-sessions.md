You have spent weeks on the bench. You have meticulously replaced every dried-out electrolytic capacitor in a [Marantz 2270 restoration guide](/guides/marantz-2270-restoration), scrubbed the oxidation off every potentiometer with [DeoxIT cleaning guide](/guides/deoxit-usage-guide), and reset the DC offset to factory specifications. Now comes the moment that justifies the neck strain and the solder fumes: the first critical listening session. But if you reach for a worn-out, G-plus copy of a generic greatest hits compilation, you are doing your restoration a massive disservice. A vintage system is only as good as the signal you feed it. To truly understand if your work has restored the original transient response and stereo imaging of a high-end piece of 1970s iron, you need records that were engineered with obsessive attention to detail. In our experience, the right approach makes all the difference.

**SAFETY WARNING:** Before you begin testing any vintage amplifier or receiver that you have recently opened, remember that these units contain lethal mains voltages. Always ensure that large filter capacitors are fully discharged before touching internal components. If you are performing a first-time power-up after a major service, use a Dim Bulb Tester to prevent catastrophic component failure.

## Why Source Material Matters for Vintage Gear
High-fidelity restoration requires high-fidelity testing. Using a compressed digital stream or a scratched record won't reveal the nuances of your component upgrades. To hear the difference those new film capacitors made in the signal path, you need a noise floor that is as low as possible.

Often cited as the pinnacle of studio production, this album is essential for checking channel separation and clarity. The title track, in particular, features complex percussion and woodwinds that will highlight any lingering distortion in your preamp stage.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**What is the best way to clean records before a demo?**
Always use a vacuum-based cleaning system or a high-quality microfiber brush to ensure no dust interferes with the stylus, as surface noise can mask the subtle improvements of a restoration.

**Should I use original pressings or modern remasters?**
While original pressings offer historical accuracy, high-quality 180g remasters often provide a lower noise floor, which is preferable for testing the signal-to-noise ratio of your equipment.
